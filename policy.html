Policy Recommendations

Data-backed zoning, building code, and incentive strategies to expand first-time homebuyer access in El Paso

Executive Summary

El Paso's first-time homebuyer market is healthy but faces three structural constraints: (1) geographic concentration in three zip codes, creating supply scarcity; (2) reliance on government-backed lending due to limited down-payment savings; and (3) affordability tightening for regional in-movers (3.09x price-to-income ratio). This policy brief outlines targeted interventions in zoning, building code, and financial accessibility to expand homeownership access for the 75.1% of buyers earning below $150k annually.

I. Zoning & Land Use Reforms

Geographic concentration (58.6% of purchases in three zip codes) creates supply scarcity and price inflation. Zoning reforms should expand buildable land and diversify housing product types in underutilized but accessible areas.

A. Upzone Underutilized Corridors (79912, 79936, and areas east of 79938)

Recommendation: Rezone corridors to allow mixed-use and multifamily development

Current constraint: Single-family zoning dominates El Paso, limiting supply of entry-point affordable units. Concentrated first-time buyer demand (27% in 79928 alone) inflates prices in high-demand areas.

Action: Rezone secondary corridors (79912, 79936, commercial strips along Montana Ave, Yarbrough) to allow:

  • Mixed-use residential (MUR-2/3): 2–4 unit buildings on standard residential lots, reducing per-unit land costs
  • Infill multifamily (RM-1): 6–12 unit buildings on commercial/industrial parcels to increase density
  • Transit-oriented development (TOD) overlays: Streamlined zoning near transit nodes to reduce approval timelines
Data support: 79912 and 79936 combined represent 15% of market (127 purchases). Expanding upzoning in these areas by 30–50% unit capacity would relieve pressure on 79928 (currently 27% of market) and increase total supply by ~40–60 units annually.

B. Legalize Accessory Dwelling Units (ADUs)

Recommendation: Streamline ADU approval in single-family neighborhoods

Current constraint: Restrictive ADU regulations limit supply of affordable rental units and owner-occupant financing options. Young buyers (50% are ages 25–34) often seek flexible housing arrangements.

Action: Adopt by-right ADU approval for single-family parcels with:

  • Up to 800 sqft detached units (reduced parking minimums: 0–1 space)
  • Exemption from conditional use permits in designated neighborhoods
  • Reduced impact fees (50% waiver) for ADUs priced at <120% AMI
Data support: Median first-time buyer age is 33 with median income $97k. ADU legalization would create ~200–300 rental units annually at $800–$1,000/month in moderate-income neighborhoods, providing pathways to ownership for renters unable to save down payments.

C. Reduce Minimum Parking Requirements

Recommendation: Cut parking minimums in infill/multifamily zones by 50%

Current constraint: Parking minimums (typically 1.5–2 spaces per unit) add $10,000–$20,000 per unit to construction costs. This directly reduces affordability and delays projects.

Action: Adopt tiered parking minimums:

  • Downtown/core areas: 0.5 spaces per unit (vs. current 1.5)
  • Infill corridors: 0.75 spaces per unit (vs. current 1.5–2)
  • Transit-adjacent: 0.25 spaces per unit + bike parking mandate
Cost impact: Reducing parking by 1 space per unit saves ~$12,000 per unit (15–20% of total construction cost in El Paso). For a 4-unit building, total savings = $48,000, translating to $300–$400 monthly mortgage savings for entry-point buyers.

D. Create Opportunity Zones for Affordable Housing

Recommendation: Tax increment and fee waivers in designated corridors

Target areas: 79912, 79936, underdeveloped parcels east of 79938.

Action: Designate opportunity zones with:

  • Impact fee waivers: 100% waiver for units <120% AMI; 50% for <150% AMI
  • Property tax abatement: 5-year freeze on assessed value increases for new construction
  • Development incentive overlays: Density bonuses (+15% units) for projects with 20%+ affordability
Data support: Impact fees in El Paso range $6,000–$12,000 per unit. Waiving fees on entry-point projects (<$250k) would reduce construction costs by 5–8%, supporting production of ~50–80 additional units annually in underutilized areas.

II. Building Code & Development Streamlining

Extended approval timelines and prescriptive code requirements inflate soft costs. Streamlining can reduce per-unit construction costs by 5–15%, directly improving affordability for buyers earning <$150k (75% of cohort).

A. Adopt Prescriptive Affordable Housing Code

Recommendation: Performance-based code for projects <150% AMI

Current constraint: Prescriptive (checklist-based) codes require exact compliance with materials/systems. Performance-based alternatives (meeting outcomes without dictating methods) reduce cost and approval time.

Action: Establish performance-based building code track for projects meeting affordability thresholds:

  • Projects with ≥50% units <150% AMI eligible for performance-based review
  • Streamlined mechanical/electrical approval (3 weeks vs. 6–8 weeks)
  • Expedited occupancy permits (same-day vs. 5-day standard)
Cost/timeline impact: Performance-based review reduces soft costs (permitting, insurance, interest during construction) by ~2–3%, or $5,000–$8,000 per unit. For a 10-unit project, total savings = $50,000–$80,000, reducing average sale price by $5,000–$8,000 per unit.

B. Standardize Utility Infrastructure

Recommendation: Pre-engineered utility networks in opportunity zones

Current constraint: Each project requires custom utility designs. Pre-approved infrastructure templates reduce engineering costs and approval timelines.

Action: Publish standard utility designs for infill corridors:

  • Water/sewer: Modular stub configurations for multi-unit infill
  • Electrical: Pre-approved panel layouts for 4–6 unit buildings
  • Stormwater: Permeable pavement/bio-swales (vs. detention ponds)
Cost impact: Custom utility design = $2,000–$4,000 per unit. Standardized designs reduce this to $500–$1,000, saving $1,500–$3,000 per unit. For El Paso's target of 50–100 units/year in opportunity zones, total savings = $75,000–$300,000 annually.

C. Expedited Permitting for Affordable Housing

Recommendation: Fast-track approval with dedicated review staff

Action: Establish 60-day approval timeline for affordable projects:

  • Dedicated affordable housing reviewer (1 FTE position)
  • Concurrent (vs. sequential) plan reviews
  • Pre-development guidance program (free consultations for developers)
Timeline/cost benefit: Reducing approval from 120+ days to 60 days saves ~9 months of carrying costs (insurance, utilities, property taxes, interest). For a $1.2M project, this saves ~$45,000–$60,000 (4–6 months of holding costs at 6% financing).

III. Down-Payment Assistance & Financing Programs

77.3% of first-time buyers rely on government-backed lending (FHA 43.6%, VA 33.7%), indicating down-payment capacity is the binding constraint. Targeted assistance programs directly expand homeownership access.

A. City-Funded Down-Payment Assistance Program

Recommendation: Graduated grant program for households <150% AMI

Program structure:

  • Income ≤100% AMI (~$65k): $10,000 grant (covers 5% down on $200k property)
  • Income 100–120% AMI ($65k–$78k): $7,500 grant
  • Income 120–150% AMI ($78k–$97k): $5,000 grant

Funding model: $2M annual allocation ($20k × 100 households = 133 units/year)

Data support: Median first-time buyer income is $97k. 46.5% earn $50k–$100k. A $10,000 grant represents 10% of median down-payment need and would enable 130–180 additional first-time buyers annually (13–18% market expansion).

B. Community Land Trust (CLT) Pilot

Recommendation: Separate land ownership to reduce purchase price

Model: CLT owns land; homeowner owns structure. Reduces effective purchase price by 20–30%.

Pilot structure:

  • Target: 50 units over 3 years in 79928 (highest concentration area)
  • City provides $1M land acquisition seed funding
  • Homebuyers purchase structure only (reduced price); lease land at $150–$250/month
  • Permanent affordability: Resale price capped at formula tied to area AMI
Cost/benefit: Median land cost in El Paso = $30,000–$50,000 (11–16% of total purchase price). Separating land through CLT reduces buyer purchase price by $30,000–$50,000, equivalent to removing $1,500–$2,500 from annual mortgage payments (enabling lower-income qualification).

C. Employer-Matched Down-Payment Savings

Recommendation: Tax incentives for employer-matched down-payment programs

Action: Create local property tax abatement (5 years) for employers offering down-payment matching (e.g., employer matches 50% of employee savings, up to $5,000/year).

Data support: 73.9% of first-time buyers are from Texas, with majority employed locally. City can incentivize major employers (healthcare, tech, manufacturing) to offer matching programs, targeting employees earning $50k–$100k (46.5% of cohort). Employer matching accelerates down-payment timeline by 2–3 years.

D. Shared Equity Purchase Program

Recommendation: City retains equity stake, buyer co-owns with city

Structure: For properties $150k–$250k, city funds 25% purchase price as equity co-owner. Buyer finances 75% via mortgage.

Example:

  • Purchase price: $220,000
  • City investment: $55,000 (25%)
  • Buyer mortgage: $165,000 (75%)
  • Buyer down payment: $0 required
  • Upon resale: City reclaims original $55k + pro-rata appreciation (e.g., 25% of gain)
Data support: 44% of purchases are in $150k–$250k range. Shared equity model eliminates down-payment barrier for this tier, enabling FHA-ineligible buyers (credit score <580, high DTI) to access homeownership. Anticipated capacity: 30–50 units/year.

Conclusion

El Paso's first-time homebuyer market is robust and locally driven (73.9% Texas-origin). The challenge is not demand, but supply expansion and affordability accessibility. The policy interventions outlined above—zoning reforms to increase supply, building code streamlining to reduce costs, and down-payment assistance to improve accessibility—are complementary levers that, when implemented in combination, can expand homeownership capacity by 15–25% over three years while maintaining affordability for the 75% of buyers earning below $150k.

Success Metrics

  • Supply: 250–300 new units in opportunity zones by Year 3
  • Affordability: Maintain 2.75x price-to-income ratio for local cohort; reduce regional in-mover ratio from 3.09x to <3.0x
  • Access: 280 households assisted via down-payment programs; 30–50 CLT homes closed
  • Equity: Ensure geographic distribution outside concentrated 79928/79938/79934 markets
